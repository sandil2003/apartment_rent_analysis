Input Variables Guide

Understanding each variable for accurate predictions

Important Note

All input variables work together to predict apartment rent. More accurate inputs lead to better predictions. Use real data from your area for best results.

Year

The year for which you want to predict the rent price.

Details

  • Type: Integer (whole number)
  • Range: 2000 - 2030
  • Default: 2024

Examples

2024, 2025, 2026

Use current or future year for predictions

Community Reporting Area ID

A numeric identifier representing a specific geographic community or neighborhood area.

Details

  • Type: Numeric (decimal allowed)
  • Range: Varies by city/region
  • Purpose: Identifies location characteristics

Examples

1.0, 15.5, 32.0, 45.2

Check your city's community area maps for IDs

Number of Properties

The total count of apartment properties or units in the census tract or area.

Details

  • Type: Integer (whole number)
  • Range: 1 - 10,000+
  • Impact: More properties may indicate density

Examples

50, 150, 500, 1000

Higher values = more apartment units in area

Shape Area

The geographic area size of the census tract or neighborhood in square units (typically square meters or feet).

Details

  • Type: Numeric (large values)
  • Unit: Square meters/feet
  • Impact: Affects property density calculations

Examples

500,000; 1,000,000; 2,500,000

Larger areas typically have lower density

Shape Length

The perimeter or boundary length of the census tract or neighborhood area in linear units.

Details

  • Type: Numeric
  • Unit: Meters or feet
  • Purpose: Represents boundary extent

Examples

3,000; 5,000; 8,500; 12,000

Related to the shape complexity of the area

Mixed-Rate or Mixed-Income Apartments

Indicates whether the tract contains apartments with mixed pricing tiers or income levels (affordable + market-rate).

Details

  • Type: Binary (Yes/No)
  • Values: 0 = No, 1 = Yes
  • Impact: Affects pricing diversity

Examples

0 (No) or 1 (Yes)

Select "Yes" if area has income-restricted units

Area Encoded

A numerical encoding of the community/neighborhood name. Each unique area is assigned a unique number.

Details

  • Type: Integer
  • Range: 0 to N (number of areas)
  • Purpose: Categorical neighborhood identifier

Examples

0, 5, 12, 25, 48

Different neighborhoods have different codes

Cost Category Encoded

A numerical representation of the rent price category (e.g., Low, Medium, High, Very High).

Details

  • Type: Integer
  • Range: 0 - 4 (typically)
  • Meaning: 0=Low, 1=Medium, 2=High, etc.

Examples

0 (Low cost), 1 (Medium), 2 (High), 3 (Very High)

Based on area's typical rent levels

Change Category Encoded

Represents the year-over-year rent change trend (e.g., Decreasing, Stable, Increasing, Rapidly Increasing).

Details

  • Type: Integer
  • Range: 0 - 3 (typically)
  • Meaning: 0=Decreasing, 1=Stable, 2=Increasing

Examples

0 (Decreasing), 1 (Stable), 2 (Increasing)

Based on historical rent trends

Automatically Calculated Features

These features are automatically computed from your inputs:

Properties Per Area

Formula: Number of Properties ÷ (Shape Area + 1)

Measures property density in the area

Year Since 2000

Formula: Year - 2000

Normalizes year data for the model

Frequently Asked Questions

Where can I find these values?

Check your city's open data portal, census bureau data, or property assessment records for geographic and demographic information.

What if I don't have exact values?

Use reasonable estimates based on similar areas. The model will still provide useful predictions, though accuracy may vary.

Can I use this for any city?

The model is trained on specific data. For best results, use data from areas similar to the training dataset.

How accurate are the predictions?

The model has an R² score of 0.9844 with an average error of $35.65, indicating high accuracy on test data.
